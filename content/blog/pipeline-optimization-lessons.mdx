This is the story of how we cut the p99 latency on our internal VIN decoding API from 800ms to 120ms, and what I learned about latency work in the process.

## The Context

The VIN API was, on paper, fine. p50 was 220ms. Most consumers were happy. But the p99 was sitting at 800ms and complaints were coming from the teams whose UX depended on tail latency &mdash; specifically, the inventory team whose page rendered VIN-decoded data inline and whose page-load metrics were getting hammered.

The first thing I did was the wrong thing: I started suggesting fixes.

"Probably the database, let's add an index."
"Maybe Postgres connection pooling is exhausted."
"Could be GC pressure."

None of these were measurements. They were guesses. To my credit I caught myself before doing any of them, but only because someone on my team asked the right question.

## Step 1: Establish a Baseline

Before changing anything, I needed a baseline. Not just an average. Averages hide everything interesting in latency work. The shape of a latency distribution matters more than its center.

I instrumented the API to emit p50, p95, and p99 percentiles every 30 seconds, broken down by endpoint and by hour-of-day. The picture that emerged was useful:

- p50 was healthy. The bulk of requests were fast.
- p95 was elevated but acceptable.
- p99 was a wall &mdash; not a long tail, but a step function. There was a population of requests that consistently took 600-900ms, and they were not random.

That second observation reframed the problem. This wasn't "things are slow." This was "a specific class of requests is slow." That's a much more tractable problem.

## Step 2: Profile

I expected the database to be the bottleneck. That was the team consensus and it was the natural assumption &mdash; databases are usually the slow thing.

I added timing spans around every external call: Postgres, Redis, NHTSA, manufacturer APIs. I logged the breakdown for the next 24 hours of slow requests.

The actual breakdown for a 750ms request:

- Postgres: 18ms
- Redis: 4ms
- NHTSA API: 680ms
- Manufacturer API: timed out and fell back to NHTSA, adding another 50ms

Postgres was not the bottleneck. Postgres was *fast*. The problem was that on cache misses, we were synchronously calling NHTSA, and NHTSA's p99 was *itself* 700ms. We were inheriting their entire latency distribution at exactly the moments we cared about most.

This is why measurement matters. A two-week index optimization on Postgres would not have moved p99 by even one millisecond.

## Step 3: Design the Caching Layers

With the actual bottleneck identified, the design became clear. We needed to make sure the slow path (cache miss into NHTSA) was hit as rarely as possible.

I designed two cache tiers.

**L1 in-memory LRU.** Per-instance, ~10K entries. Sub-millisecond access. The motivation was the batch endpoint &mdash; teams sending 100 VINs in a single request shouldn't be paying for 100 Redis round-trips.

**L2 Redis.** Shared across instances, much larger capacity, TTLs tiered by data source. NHTSA decodes are effectively immutable, so a 7-day TTL is conservative. Manufacturer data is more volatile, so 30 days. Internal DB hits, 90 days.

I also added a weekly cache-warming job that pre-populates the top 10K VINs from the prior week's traffic. This was specifically for Monday mornings, when the cache was cold and the traffic spike was real.

The change I almost made and didn't: a CDN. Latency improvement at the edge wouldn't have helped, because our consumers are internal services, not browsers. The bottleneck was upstream, not on the wire.

## Step 4: Implement and Measure After Each Change

I rolled out one change at a time and measured between each. This is the tedious part, and it's the part that engineers most often skip.

- Adding L1 alone: p99 down to 480ms. (The batch endpoint moved a lot.)
- Adding L2 (Redis) on top: p99 down to 220ms.
- Adding the weekly warm-up: p99 down to 140ms.
- Increasing L1 size after measuring its hit rate: p99 down to 120ms.

Each change had a measurable effect. If a change had *not* moved the metric, I'd want to know why before continuing &mdash; that's a sign that my model of the system is wrong.

## Step 5: The Final Numbers

After six weeks of work:

- p50: 220ms &rarr; 25ms
- p95: 480ms &rarr; 60ms
- p99: 800ms &rarr; 120ms
- Upstream API call volume: down 78%

The upstream cost reduction was a nice side effect. The win we cared about was the p99.

## Lessons

**Measure first. Always.** Two weeks of "obvious" optimization on the wrong subsystem would have produced zero improvement and a more brittle codebase.

**The shape of latency matters more than the average.** "p99 is 800ms" is the actionable framing. "Average response time is 280ms" is the framing that hides every interesting bug.

**Cache invalidation has to be designed up front, not retrofitted.** Every TTL I added was tied to a documented assumption about the data's freshness. Every layer had a written answer to "what happens when this becomes stale?" That documentation has paid for itself many times over since.

**One change at a time, always with a measurement between.** Stacking three changes and then measuring is how you end up arguing for six months about which one actually helped.

The bigger lesson, the one I keep coming back to: most performance problems are not what you think they are. The discipline of measuring before doing is the difference between two weeks of useful work and two weeks of confident motion in the wrong direction.
